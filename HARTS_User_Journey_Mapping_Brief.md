# HARTS Website — User Journey Mapping Brief

**Company type:** Services / Consulting / Agency
**Primary website goals:** Lead generation + direct enquiries + brand authority + information for existing clients
**Document purpose:** A complete, self-contained reference that Claude Code can read once and use to architect, design, and build the HARTS website around real user journeys instead of guessing at structure.

**How to use this document:**
1. Read Sections 1–3 to understand the concept of user journey mapping in a services context.
2. Use Section 4 to identify HARTS's actual users (personas).
3. Use Section 5 to build the stage-by-stage journey map.
4. Use Section 6 as a direct implementation checklist for the website itself.
5. Use Section 7 for the measurement plan.

## 1. What Is User Journey Mapping (For a Services Business)?

A **user journey map** is a stage-by-stage representation of every interaction a person has with HARTS — from the moment they first realise they have a problem, through researching, shortlisting, enquiring, becoming a client, and (ideally) referring others.

For a services / consulting / agency business, the journey looks very different from a SaaS or e-commerce journey in five important ways:

1. **The sale is high-trust, not high-feature.** Nobody buys consulting because of a feature list. They buy because they believe HARTS understands their problem and can be trusted with it.
2. **The decision window is long.** Buyers often research silently for weeks or months before making any contact. The website is a salesperson working 24/7 while nobody is watching.
3. **The primary conversion is a conversation, not a purchase.** The website's job is to make the user *willing to talk*, not to close a deal.
4. **Authority compounds.** Every article, case study, and credential builds a trust account the user draws from on the decision day.
5. **The buyer is rarely alone.** Services are often bought by committee — a champion finds HARTS, then has to convince a boss, a finance team, and possibly a board.

A journey map for HARTS therefore answers a different question than for a product company: *"At every stage, what does this person need to see, read, or feel before they're willing to (a) come back, (b) share with a colleague, or (c) reach out to us?"*

---

## 2. The Core Components of the HARTS Journey Map

Every robust journey map contains seven layers. The HARTS map must include all seven.

1. **Persona** — *who* is moving through the journey.
2. **Stages** — the phases of the relationship. For services: Awareness → Research → Shortlist → Enquiry → Proposal → Engagement → Delivery → Advocacy.
3. **User goals per stage** — what the user is trying to achieve in their work/life, not what HARTS wants them to do.
4. **Touchpoints** — every place the user encounters HARTS (search results, LinkedIn, homepage, case study page, intro call, proposal, kickoff meeting, project portal).
5. **Actions** — what the user actually does.
6. **Thoughts & emotions** — what they're thinking and feeling. This is what turns a flowchart into a journey *map*.
7. **Pain points & opportunities** — friction at each stage and the specific website intervention that removes it.

For a services business, add an essential eighth layer:

8. **Backstage / system response** — what the website, CRM, and HARTS team must do behind the scenes (route the lead, send a follow-up, schedule a call, store the proposal). Services businesses lose more deals to poor follow-through than to poor websites.

## 3. Types of Journey Maps — Pick the Right One

There are four standard variants. For HARTS, build two and consult a third:

**(a) Current-state map (build if HARTS already has web traffic).** Documents how visitors experience HARTS today. Pull from analytics, sales-call recordings, enquiry-form data, and lost-deal post-mortems. Without it, redesigns are guesses.

**(b) Future-state map (build — this is the primary artifact).** The ideal journey the new website is designed *toward*. Every page, component, and CTA traces back to a row in this map.

**(c) Day-in-the-life map (consult when writing the homepage).** Shows the user's whole working day, including the moments where a problem HARTS solves arises. This tells you when and why a person would think *"we need help with this"* — which is the seed of every headline on the site.

**(d) Service blueprint (build once enquiries start flowing).** Extends the journey map with everything happening backstage — who at HARTS responds to enquiries, who runs the intro call, how proposals are produced, what happens at kickoff. For a services business, this is where most operational improvements live.

## 4. Personas — Define These Before You Map Anything

A journey map without a persona is fiction. For a services business, build **3–4 personas**. Suggested starting set:

### Persona 1 — The Champion (most common visitor)
The person who feels the pain, does the research, and pushes internally for HARTS to be hired. Often a department head or senior manager, not the ultimate decision-maker.

### Persona 2 — The Economic Buyer
The person who signs the contract. Usually senior (Director, VP, Founder, CEO). Visits the website *after* the Champion has shortlisted HARTS, looking for reasons to disqualify, not reasons to choose.

### Persona 3 — The Gatekeeper / Influencer
Legal, procurement, IT security, finance. Looking for risk: insurance, references, data handling, contract terms. May never speak to HARTS directly but can kill a deal silently.

### Persona 4 — The Existing Client
Already working with HARTS. Comes back for case studies to share internally, to find a specific consultant's bio, to read a recent insight, or to refer a peer. Often forgotten in website design — and they're the most valuable visitors.

### Per-persona template

For each persona, document:

| Field | What to capture |
|---|---|
| Name & role | "Anjali, Head of Operations, mid-market manufacturer" |
| Seniority | Affects tone and proof required |
| Trigger event | What just happened in their work that sent them searching |
| Goals | What they want to achieve in their actual job |
| Frustrations | What's currently failing them — the wedge HARTS uses |
| Research behaviour | Where they look: Google, LinkedIn, peer referrals, industry publications |
| Decision authority | Can they hire HARTS alone, or who else must approve? |
| Risk concerns | What scares them about hiring a consultancy |
| Preferred channels | Email, phone, LinkedIn DM, intro call, written proposal |
| Reading style | Skimmer or deep-reader; affects content density |

### The Jobs-to-Be-Done statement

For each persona, write one sentence in this format:
> *"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."*

Example: *"When my leadership team keeps blaming each other for missed targets, I want an outside partner who can diagnose what's actually broken, so I can fix it without becoming the bad guy."*

This sentence is the seed of every headline, hero image, case-study angle, and CTA on the HARTS site.

## 5. The HARTS Journey Map — Stage by Stage

The full eight-stage framework for a consulting / services business. Each row below is what Claude Code must answer for HARTS specifically before designing the corresponding section of the site.

### Stage 1 — Awareness
The user has a problem but hasn't yet thought of hiring outside help.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Understand what they're dealing with; name the problem |
| Touchpoints | Google search, LinkedIn post, peer conversation, industry article, podcast |
| Actions | Searches generic terms ("how to fix X"), reads thought leadership, follows experts on LinkedIn |
| Thoughts | "Is this a real problem worth solving? Have others dealt with it? Is it just us?" |
| Emotions | Curious, frustrated, sometimes embarrassed |
| Pain points | Generic advice, surface-level content, no clear authority |
| **Website implication** | **Insights / Thought leadership hub:** problem-first articles, frameworks, opinion pieces. The article — not the homepage — is the entry point. Every article must be standalone-useful even if the reader never explores further. Author bylines with photos and credentials build authority. |

### Stage 2 — Research
They now suspect outside help might be the answer.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Understand what kind of help exists; what shape it takes; rough cost expectations |
| Touchpoints | Homepage, services pages, "about us", insights articles, founder/partner LinkedIn |
| Actions | Reads service descriptions, scans case studies, looks up team bios, checks LinkedIn for mutual connections |
| Thoughts | "What do firms like HARTS actually do? Are they serious? Do they look like people I'd want to work with?" |
| Emotions | Evaluative, curious, slightly cautious |
| Pain points | Vague service descriptions, stock photography, no real people visible, generic "we partner with you" language |
| **Website implication** | **Specificity + humanity:** services pages that describe real engagements (not "strategic transformation"), team page with real photos and named experience, "how we work" page showing methodology, clear practice-area structure. |

### Stage 3 — Shortlist
HARTS is one of 3–5 firms being seriously considered.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Find proof HARTS has solved their exact problem before |
| Touchpoints | Case studies, client logos, testimonials, sector/industry pages, awards & credentials |
| Actions | Reads case studies in detail, looks for clients similar to them, checks credentials, reads founder/partner profiles |
| Thoughts | "Have they worked with someone like us? Can I picture them in our boardroom? Will my boss respect this firm?" |
| Emotions | Cautious, looking for reasons to disqualify, building a mental shortlist |
| Pain points | Anonymised case studies, no named clients, no industry match, generic testimonials |
| **Website implication** | **Proof stack:** case studies organised by sector and by problem-type, named client logos (with permission), outcome metrics ("reduced cycle time 40%"), testimonials with name + role + photo + company, awards/accreditations, published thought leadership signalling expertise. |

### Stage 4 — Enquiry
They've decided to make contact.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Get a conversation, ideally on their terms (email vs call), without over-committing |
| Touchpoints | Contact page, "Book an intro call" CTA, email, LinkedIn DM to a partner |
| Actions | Fills out enquiry form, books a call, emails a partner directly, sends a message via LinkedIn |
| Thoughts | "What will happen when I submit this? Will I get hard-sold? How quickly will they reply?" |
| Emotions | Hopeful but exposed; reaching out to a consultancy feels like admitting you need help |
| Pain points | Long forms, no expectation-setting, slow replies, generic auto-responders |
| **Website implication** | **Low-friction enquiry stack:** short enquiry form (name, company, email, "what's on your mind"), multiple contact paths (form, email, calendar link, phone), explicit expectation-setting ("a partner will reply within one working day"), human auto-response, contact-page bios showing who they'll actually meet. |

### Stage 5 — Proposal / Evaluation
They've spoken with HARTS; now multiple stakeholders are reviewing.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Sell HARTS internally to their boss, board, or committee |
| Touchpoints | Proposal document, follow-up emails, the website (re-visited with colleagues), references |
| Actions | Forwards the website to internal stakeholders, re-reads case studies, checks security/insurance/legal pages, requests references |
| Thoughts | "Can I defend this choice if it goes wrong? Does this firm look credible to my CFO?" |
| Emotions | Champion is invested; gatekeepers are skeptical |
| Pain points | No content for committee members; nothing aimed at the CFO/CTO/legal reviewer |
| **Website implication** | **Buyer-committee content:** an "about HARTS" page with the kind of firm credentials a CFO needs (years operating, jurisdictions, insurance, accreditations), a "working with us" page covering contracting/IP/data handling, downloadable capability statement (PDF), team page proving senior involvement. |

### Stage 6 — Engagement
The contract is signed. The first 30 days set the tone for everything.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | See immediate professionalism; feel they made the right choice |
| Touchpoints | Welcome email, kickoff meeting, project portal, shared docs |
| Actions | Logs in to project space, meets the team, reviews kickoff materials |
| Thoughts | "Did we hire the right firm? Will the people I met be the people doing the work?" |
| Emotions | Excited, slightly anxious |
| Pain points | Bait-and-switch (selling partners disappearing after signing), unclear next steps, slow start |
| **Website implication** | **Client portal entry point + onboarding content:** a logged-in client area (even a simple one), a "what to expect in week 1" page, easy access to the team they met during sales. |

### Stage 7 — Delivery
The work is happening.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Stay informed; access deliverables; share progress internally |
| Touchpoints | Project portal, regular reports, status updates |
| Actions | Logs in, downloads deliverables, shares with colleagues |
| **Website implication** | **Client-only resources area:** secure document access, reading list / further insights tailored to engagement type, team contact details visible. |

### Stage 8 — Advocacy
Happy clients become a growth channel — and for services firms, referrals are *the* primary growth channel.

| Layer | Detail |
|---|---|
| User goal | Help peers facing similar problems; get associated with a respected firm |
| Touchpoints | LinkedIn, industry events, referral conversations, the HARTS website (used to share) |
| Actions | Posts about HARTS, makes a referral introduction, agrees to a case study, speaks at a HARTS event |
| **Website implication** | **Shareable assets + named case studies:** case studies the client can proudly send to peers, easy LinkedIn-share buttons on insights articles, a "refer a colleague" path, an alumni/community page if it exists, public events page. |

## 6. Implementation Checklist for the HARTS Website

This is what Claude Code should build against.

### 6.1 Site architecture

Group pages by **journey stage**, not by org chart. Recommended structure:

**Awareness / authority layer**
- `/` (Home)
- `/insights` or `/thinking` — article hub
- `/insights/{article-slug}`

**Research layer**
- `/services` — overview
- `/services/{practice-area}` — one per service line
- `/how-we-work` — methodology
- `/about` — firm story, values
- `/team` — people index
- `/team/{person-slug}` — individual bios

**Shortlist layer**
- `/case-studies` — index, filterable by sector + service
- `/case-studies/{slug}` — individual case study
- `/sectors/{industry}` — industry-specific landing pages
- `/credentials` — awards, accreditations, memberships

**Enquiry layer**
- `/contact`
- `/contact/{office-location}` if multi-office
- `/book-a-call` — calendar link

**Buyer-committee layer (often forgotten)**
- `/working-with-us` — contracting, IP, confidentiality, data handling
- `/security` if relevant
- A downloadable capability statement (PDF) on the about page

**Client / advocacy layer**
- `/clients` — login
- `/events`
- `/careers` (talent journey is its own map, but lives here)

Every page must declare in its design-doc header: **which persona, which stage, which one primary CTA, which success metric.**

### 6.2 Page-level requirements

**Homepage**
- Hero headline = the type of problems HARTS solves, in the client's language. Not "Strategic advisory for ambitious organisations" — something like "[Industry] leaders hire us when [specific situation]."
- Sub-headline = one sentence on the outcome HARTS delivers.
- Primary CTA = "Book an intro call" or "Start a conversation" — not "Learn more."
- Secondary CTA = "See our work" linking to case studies.
- Above the fold: a single proof point (years operating, named clients, or a numeric outcome).
- A "what we do" section structured by client problem, not by service-line jargon.
- A "who we help" section showing 3–4 sector cards.
- A featured case study with named client and outcome metric.
- A "meet the partners" strip with 3–5 senior faces — humanity above polish.
- A featured insight to demonstrate current thinking.
- Footer with full nav, office locations, contact, social, legal.

**Services pages (one per practice area)**
- Headline = the outcome, not the service name.
- Opening paragraph = the situation a client is in when they hire HARTS for this.
- "What this looks like in practice" section — describe real engagement shapes (3-month diagnostic, 6-month transformation, ongoing advisory).
- Linked case studies on this practice area.
- The named partners who lead this practice.
- CTA: "Talk to {partner name}" — a specific person, not a generic form.

**Case studies (the workhorses of a consulting site)**
- Standard structure every time:
  1. Client name + sector + size (or anonymised tier if confidentiality required)
  2. Situation — what was going wrong
  3. Approach — what HARTS did, with enough specificity to be credible
  4. Outcome — numeric where possible
  5. Client quote — named, with photo
- Filter the index by sector AND by service so visitors can find a study that matches *both* their industry and their problem type.

**Team / About**
- Real photos, not stock.
- Each bio: current role, professional background, signature engagement types, one human detail.
- LinkedIn link for every person.
- Senior partners should have direct email/booking links.

**Contact / Enquiry**
- Maximum 5 fields. Each extra field measurably drops conversion.
- Multiple paths: form, direct partner emails, calendar link, phone.
- Explicit expectation: "A partner replies within one working day."
- Human-toned auto-response, signed by a real person.
- Confirmation page shows what happens next.

**Insights / Articles**
- Author bylines with photo, role, and LinkedIn link.
- Reading time estimate.
- Related articles by topic, not by date.
- One contextual CTA at the end matched to the article's stage (awareness-stage article → newsletter; near-decision article → book a call).
- Share buttons that pre-fill LinkedIn well (this is where services content actually travels).

### 6.3 Component-level requirements

- **Top nav:** Services, Sectors, Case Studies, Insights, About, Contact (primary CTA button).
- **Persistent secondary CTA** on every page — usually "Start a conversation" in the footer ribbon.
- **Trust elements** (logos, accreditations) repeated on enquiry-stage pages, not just home.
- **No aggressive chat pop-ups.** Services buyers are deliberate; pop-ups feel desperate and undermine premium positioning.
- **Newsletter signup** offered on insights pages, never in a modal.
- **Performance:** Largest Contentful Paint under 2.5s, Interaction to Next Paint under 200ms. A slow site signals a slow firm.
- **Mobile-first** — most early research happens on phones, especially LinkedIn-sourced traffic.
- **Accessibility:** WCAG 2.2 AA minimum. Clients in regulated sectors will check this.
- **Print stylesheet** for case studies — they get printed and circulated more than you'd think.

### 6.4 Content tone rules

- Write in the **second person ("you")** to the reader; refer to HARTS in the **first person plural ("we")**. Avoid corporate third-person ("HARTS partners with...").
- Lead with **outcomes and situations**, not service categories.
- Replace consulting jargon: "synergies", "transformation", "world-class", "best-in-class", "trusted partner", "thought leaders", "robust", "seamless" — all banned. They signal a lack of specificity and erode credibility.
- Every numeric claim must be sourced or removed.
- Match the **register** of the client. A consultancy serving family businesses writes very differently from one serving listed-company boards. Pick one and commit.
- Never use AI-generated stock imagery for the team. Real photos only — even imperfect ones outperform polished fakes.

## 7. Measurement — How to Know the Journey Works

A journey map is a hypothesis. Validate it with data.

### 7.1 Events to instrument at minimum

- Page-view per stage (so awareness-stage vs enquiry-stage traffic can be separated in reporting).
- Scroll depth (25/50/75/100%) on long pages — especially case studies.
- CTA clicks tagged by location (hero, mid-page, footer, sticky).
- Form starts vs form submits (the gap is the friction).
- Outbound clicks on partner LinkedIn profiles, email links, calendar links.
- Insights article completion + share clicks.
- Returning-visitor identification — services buyers return many times before enquiring.
- Multi-page sessions involving a case study + contact page (high intent).

### 7.2 Stage-by-stage KPIs

| Stage | Primary KPI | Secondary KPIs |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Organic sessions, insights article reach | Article completion rate, LinkedIn shares |
| Research | Services & team page views per session | Pages-per-session, returning visitors |
| Shortlist | Case-study views by qualified visitors | Multi-case-study sessions, sector-page views |
| Enquiry | Enquiry-form submits + calendar bookings | Form completion rate, partner-email clicks |
| Proposal | Sales-pipeline data (not website data) | Time from enquiry → meeting → proposal |
| Engagement | Client-portal logins | First-week activity |
| Delivery | Client-resource downloads | Repeat logins |
| Advocacy | Referral enquiries, named case-study consents | Net new referral revenue, LinkedIn mentions |

### 7.3 Continuous improvement loop

Every quarter:
1. Pull the funnel above.
2. Identify the one stage with the worst stage-to-stage conversion.
3. Ship two changes that address it (A/B test where volume allows).
4. Update the journey map with what was learned.

For a services firm, also do this annually: **interview the last 5 won clients and the last 5 lost prospects.** Ask what role the website played, what reassured them, what didn't. This qualitative input is more valuable than any analytics number.

## 9. Five Things Most Consulting Websites Get Wrong

Call-outs for Claude Code to actively avoid.

1. **Homepage talks about HARTS, not the visitor.** "We are a leading consultancy..." is the #1 conversion-killer. The first words on the page should be about the visitor's situation.
2. **Case studies are anonymised into uselessness.** "A leading European bank" is unverifiable and therefore worthless. Name clients wherever permission can be obtained; offer a named version on request even when public anonymisation is required.
3. **Team page is hidden or thin.** Buyers want to know who they'll meet. The team page should be one click from anywhere on the site and should feature real photographs.
4. **No content for the gatekeeper.** Procurement, legal, and finance reviewers can silently kill a deal. A "working with us" page covering contracting, insurance, IP, and data handling pre-empts this.
5. **Insights are dated and abandoned.** A "latest insights" section with the most recent article from 14 months ago actively hurts credibility. Either commit to publishing or remove the section.

## Appendix — Quick-Reference Vocabulary

- **Touchpoint** — any moment of contact between user and HARTS (page, email, ad, call, proposal, meeting).
- **Channel** — the medium (web, email, phone, LinkedIn, in-person).
- **Friction** — anything that slows or stops progress to the next stage.
- **Champion** — the internal advocate who pushes for HARTS to be hired.
- **Economic buyer** — the person who actually signs the contract.
- **Gatekeeper** — procurement, legal, IT, finance — people who can block a deal without endorsing one.
- **Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)** — the underlying outcome a client "hires" HARTS to achieve.
- **Service blueprint** — a journey map plus all backstage staff/system actions.
- **TOFU/MOFU/BOFU** — Top/Middle/Bottom of funnel; rough equivalents of Awareness/Research/Enquiry stages.
- **CTA** — call to action; the specific next step offered on a page.
